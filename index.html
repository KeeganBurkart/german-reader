Case endings: who does what to whom
German word order is flexible because the article carries the grammar. Read the article, not the position.
mascfemneutplural
Subject (nom)der / eindie / einedas / eindie
Direct obj (acc)den / einendie / einedas / eindie
Indirect obj (dat)demderdemden +n

Den Mann beißt der Hund = the dog bites the man — den marks the victim even in first position. Only masculine visibly distinguishes nom/acc; when in doubt, first noun is usually (not always!) the subject.

Dative-only prepositions: aus, bei, mit, nach, seit, von, zu. Accusative-only: durch, für, gegen, ohne, um. Two-way (an, auf, in, über, unter…): dative = location, accusative = movement toward.
Verb position: bracket structure
Main clause: conjugated verb is always slot 2; everything else (infinitive, participle, prefix) piles up at the end. Read slot 2, then jump to the end, then fill in the middle.
Ich habe gestern in Wien einen alten Freund getroffen.

Subordinate clause (after dass, weil, obwohl, wenn, der/die/das as relative…): conjugated verb goes to the very end.
…, weil ich ihn seit Jahren nicht gesehen habe.

Reading strategy: when a sentence feels endless, find the verbs at the end first — they're the skeleton.
Separable verbs: the #1 reading trap
Der Zug fährt um 8 Uhr in Wien ab. — the verb is abfahren (depart), not fahren. The prefix hides at the end of the clause.

If a sentence ends in a lone an, auf, aus, ab, ein, mit, nach, vor, zu, zurück, weiter, statt… — glue it onto the main verb and look that word up.

Common shifts: kommen (come) → ankommen (arrive), bekommen (receive!); stellen (put) → vorstellen (introduce/imagine); hören (hear) → aufhören (stop).

Perfect tense: ge—t / ge—en at the clause end (gemacht, gesehen). Strip ge- and the ending to find the stem. No ge- on verbs in -ieren (studiert) or with be-, ver-, er- prefixes (verstanden).
werden: three different jobs
patternmeaning
werden + adjective/nounto become — Es wird kalt.
werden + infinitive (at end)future — Ich werde kommen.
werden + participle (at end)passive — Das Museum wird renoviert. (is being renovated)
wurde … gebaut = "was built" — extremely common in museum placards and Wikipedia.
Konjunktiv I: news-speak for "allegedly"
Journalists use special verb forms to mark reported speech — "he said that…" without repeating "said":
Der Minister sagte, es gebe keine Krise. Man habe alles geprüft. Die Lage sei stabil.

sei (=is, reportedly), habe (=has), gebe, könne, müsse, werde — when you see these, the paper is quoting someone, not asserting fact. Don't confuse sei with seit (since).
Konjunktiv II: would / were
wäre (would be), hätte (would have), würde + infinitive (would …), könnte (could), müsste (would have to), sollte (should). Umlaut on a past form ≈ hypothetical: Wenn ich Zeit hätte, würde ich bleiben.
The monster in front of the noun (extended participle)
German stuffs whole clauses between the article and its noun:
die im Jahr 1869 eröffnete Staatsoper = the State Opera, (which was) opened in 1869.

Fix: when an article is followed by something that isn't its noun, skip ahead to the next noun, then read the stuffing as a relative clause. Article … noun first, middle second.
Compound nouns: read right-to-left
The last element is the actual thing; everything before it describes it. Weihnachts·markt·stand = a stand, at a market, for Christmas. Linking letters -s-/-n- (Arbeitszeit, Straßenbahn) belong to neither word — chop and look up the parts. Your 4,214 words unlock tens of thousands of compounds this way.
